In the Grand Library, you learned how the concierge finds answers: she sends the guest's question to the library, which returns the most relevant pages from the guest book (context chunks). The concierge reads those pages and crafts a reply.

New concept: Confidence scoring — three methods, weighted together, reported but never blocking. The Night Watchman reads the concierge's reply AFTER it has been sent. He checks it against the guest book pages. He writes a report. He never stops the reply from going out.

Three Investigators — The Hallucination Patrol

Every time the concierge writes a reply, three investigators read it. Each one has a different way of checking whether the reply matches the guest book. They never talk to the concierge. They never stop the reply. They just write their scores on a card and hand it to the Night Watchman.

Cookie Monster — The Word-By-Word Checker

"Me take EVERY word in the reply... me check if that word is ALSO in the guest book. Om nom nom."

Cookie Monster is thorough but literal. He breaks the reply into individual words. He breaks the guest book into individual words. He counts how many words overlap. If the concierge paraphrased something — used different words to say the same thing — Cookie Monster might miss it entirely.

Elmo — The Meaning-Code Checker

"Elmo turns the reply AND the guest book into meaning-codes, then checks how close they are!"

Elmo doesn't look at individual words. He reads the whole reply and converts it into a long list of numbers that represent its meaning. He does the same for each page of the guest book. Then he checks: how close is the reply's meaning to the closest page's meaning? Elmo catches paraphrases that Cookie Monster misses — but he can be fooled by sentences that sound similar but mean different things.

Grover — The Sentence Tracer

"Grover reads each sentence of the reply very carefully. For each sentence, he asks: can I find at least 30% of these words in the guest book? If yes, this sentence is grounded."

Grover is the most careful but the slowest. He splits the reply into individual sentences. For each sentence, he checks word overlap with the entire guest book. If a sentence has at least 30% of its words found in the guest book, Grover marks it as "grounded." His score = grounded sentences divided by total sentences.

Cookie Monster's Check — Word by Word

Cookie Monster grabs the concierge's reply in one hand and the guest book pages in the other. He breaks both into individual words — lowercase, no punctuation, just raw words. Then he counts how many of the reply's words also appear in the guest book. That's it. That's the whole check.

Cookie Monster — In the Hallway

"Me got the reply. Me got the guest book. Me smash both into little word crumbs. Om nom nom."

Cookie Monster takes the reply: "Our hotel offers luxurious spa treatments and fine dining." He breaks it into crumbs: [our, hotel, offers, luxurious, spa, treatments, and, fine, dining].

Then he takes the guest book pages and breaks THOSE into crumbs too. He pours both piles of crumbs onto the table and counts: how many of the reply's crumbs are also in the guest book pile?

His score = matching crumbs / total reply crumbs.

PYTHON def _tokenize(text: str) -> set[str]: """Break the text into word-crumbs — lowercase, no punctuation.""" return set(re.findall(r"\w+", text.lower())) # re.findall(r"\w+", ...) grabs every sequence of letters/numbers # .lower() makes everything lowercase so "Hotel" matches "hotel" # set() removes duplicates — each word appears only once
PYTHON def _compute_context_overlap(self, response_text, contexts) -> float: """Cookie Monster's check: how many reply-words are in the guest book?""" if not contexts: return 0.0 # No guest book pages? Score is zero. # Break the reply into word-crumbs response_tokens = _tokenize(response_text) if not response_tokens: return 0.0 # Empty reply? Nothing to check. # Pour ALL guest book pages into one big pile of crumbs context_tokens = set() for ctx in contexts: context_tokens.update(_tokenize(ctx)) # Count how many reply-crumbs are also in the guest book pile overlap = response_tokens & context_tokens # set intersection return len(overlap) / len(response_tokens) # Score = matching crumbs / total reply crumbs
🍪 Cookie Monster catches obvious fabrications but misses paraphrases. If the concierge says "luxurious spa treatments" but the guest book says "premium wellness services," Cookie Monster sees zero overlap for those words. The concierge said the same thing in different words — but Cookie Monster only counts exact matches. That's why he's not the only investigator. He's fast, he's simple, and he catches made-up names and numbers. But he needs help from Elmo and Grover.
"Cookie Monster counts crumbs. If the crumbs match, the reply is grounded.
If the concierge used different crumbs to say the same thing, Cookie Monster doesn't notice."

Elmo & Grover — Meaning and Sentences

Cookie Monster checks words. Elmo checks meaning. Grover checks sentences. Together, the three investigators cover each other's blind spots.

Elmo — The Meaning-Code Checker

Elmo doesn't read individual words. Elmo reads the whole reply and turns it into a long list of numbers — a meaning-code. Think of it like a fingerprint for what the reply means, not what words it uses.

Then Elmo turns each guest-book page into a meaning-code too. Now he has: one fingerprint for the reply, and one fingerprint for each page of the guest book.

Elmo holds up the reply's fingerprint next to each page's fingerprint and asks: "How similar are these two?" He measures the angle between them — closer means more similar. He picks the best match.

If the concierge said "luxurious spa treatments" and the guest book said "premium wellness services," Elmo sees that the meanings are nearly identical even though the words are completely different. That's what Cookie Monster missed.

PYTHON def _compute_semantic_similarity(self, response_text, contexts) -> float: """Elmo's check: how close is the reply's MEANING to the guest book?""" model = self._get_model() # Wake up the meaning-code brain (lazy-loaded) if model is None or not contexts: return 0.0 # No brain or no guest book? Score zero. try: import numpy as np # Turn the reply into a meaning-code (one fingerprint) resp_emb = model.encode([response_text])[0] # Turn each guest-book page into a meaning-code ctx_emb = model.encode(contexts) # Compare the reply's fingerprint to each page's fingerprint similarities = [] for emb in ctx_emb: # Cosine similarity: how close are the two meaning-codes? cos_sim = float( np.dot(resp_emb, emb) / (np.linalg.norm(resp_emb) * np.linalg.norm(emb) + 1e-10) ) similarities.append(cos_sim) # Take the BEST match — the closest guest-book page return max(similarities) if similarities else 0.0 except Exception as e: return 0.0 # If anything goes wrong, score zero
❤️ Elmo uses the same brain as the Grand Library — the SentenceTransformer model called all-MiniLM-L6-v2. Elmo only wakes the brain up the first time he needs it. After that, it stays awake for future checks. If the brain isn't available (the library is missing), Elmo gives a score of zero — he can't check meaning without his meaning-code machine.
Grover — The Sentence Tracer

Grover doesn't look at the reply as a whole. He splits the reply into individual sentences. He only keeps sentences that have at least 3 words — short fragments don't count.

For each sentence, Grover takes every word and checks: is this word somewhere in the guest book? He counts the ratio. If at least 30% of the sentence's words appear in the guest book, Grover marks that sentence as "grounded."

His final score = how many sentences were grounded / total sentences.

If the reply has 5 sentences and 4 of them pass the 30% word-overlap check, Grover's score is 4/5 = 0.80.

PYTHON def _split_sentences(text: str) -> List[str]: """Split the reply into sentences at period, exclamation, question mark.""" sentences = re.split(r"(?<=[.!?])\s+", text.strip()) return [s for s in sentences if len(s.split()) >= 3] # Only keep sentences with 3+ words — tiny fragments are noise
PYTHON def _compute_source_attribution(self, response_text, contexts) -> float: """Grover's check: how many sentences are grounded in the guest book?""" if not contexts: return 0.0 # Split the reply into sentences sentences = _split_sentences(response_text) if not sentences: return 0.0 # Pour ALL guest book pages into one pile of word-crumbs context_tokens = set() for ctx in contexts: context_tokens.update(_tokenize(ctx)) # Check each sentence: is it grounded? grounded = 0 for sentence in sentences: sent_tokens = _tokenize(sentence) if not sent_tokens: continue # What fraction of this sentence's words are in the guest book? overlap_ratio = len(sent_tokens & context_tokens) / len(sent_tokens) if overlap_ratio >= 0.3: # At least 30% overlap? Grounded! grounded += 1 # Score = grounded sentences / total sentences return grounded / len(sentences)
🔍 Why 30%? Think of it this way: a sentence like "The hotel pool is open from dusk until dawn" has 9 words. If at least 3 of those words (30%) appear somewhere in the guest book — "hotel," "pool," "open" — Grover considers the sentence grounded. It's a low bar on purpose: Grover isn't asking for a perfect match. He's asking "is there any connection at all between this sentence and the source material?"
"Elmo reads meaning. Grover reads sentences.
Together with Cookie Monster, every angle is covered."

The Final Report — Weighted Confidence

The Night Watchman sits down at his desk with three score cards — one from each investigator. He weighs them, calculates a final number, checks the traffic light, and posts the report to the tally board in the lobby. He never stops the concierge. He never blocks the response. He just writes the report.

The Night Watchman — At His Desk

The Night Watchman picks up the three cards:

Card 1, from Elmo: 50% weight. The meaning-code check catches the most. If the reply means the same thing as the guest book, that's the strongest signal.

Card 2, from Cookie Monster: 30% weight. The word-matching check catches fabricated names, invented numbers, and words that simply don't exist in the source material.

Card 3, from Grover: 20% weight. The sentence-tracing check catches ungrounded claims — whole sentences that have no basis in the guest book.

He multiplies each score by its weight, adds them up, and writes the final number on the report.

PYTHON def score_response(self, response_text, rag_contexts, user_query) -> ConfidenceResult: """The Night Watchman's final report. Weights: 30% word overlap + 50% meaning similarity + 20% sentence grounding""" # Ask each investigator for their score overlap = self._compute_context_overlap(response_text, rag_contexts) semantic = self._compute_semantic_similarity(response_text, rag_contexts) attribution = self._compute_source_attribution(response_text, rag_contexts) # Weighted sum: the Night Watchman's formula overall = 0.3 * overlap + 0.5 * semantic + 0.2 * attribution # Check the traffic light if overall >= self.high_threshold: # >= 0.7 = GREEN level = ConfidenceLevel.HIGH elif overall >= self.medium_threshold: # >= 0.4 = AMBER level = ConfidenceLevel.MEDIUM else: # < 0.4 = RED level = ConfidenceLevel.LOW # Post to the tally board in the lobby RESPONSE_CONFIDENCE.observe(overall) # Thermometer reading HALLUCINATIONS_DETECTED.labels(level=level.value).inc() # Tally mark # Write the report to the hotel log logger.info( f"Confidence score: {overall:.3f} ({level.value}) " f"[overlap={overlap:.3f}, semantic={semantic:.3f}, attribution={attribution:.3f}]" ) # Hand the completed report to whoever asked for it return ConfidenceResult( overall_score=overall, level=level, context_overlap_score=overlap, semantic_similarity_score=semantic, source_attribution_score=attribution, )

The Report Card — ConfidenceResult

The Night Watchman's report is a simple card with five fields: the overall score (a number from 0 to 1), the traffic light level (HIGH, MEDIUM, or LOW), and the three individual scores from each investigator. The card has a to_dict() method that converts it into a format anyone can read — rounded to 4 decimal places.

PYTHON @dataclass class ConfidenceResult: """The Night Watchman's report card.""" overall_score: float # 0.0 to 1.0 — the final number level: ConfidenceLevel # HIGH, MEDIUM, or LOW context_overlap_score: float # Cookie Monster's score semantic_similarity_score: float # Elmo's score source_attribution_score: float # Grover's score def to_dict(self) -> Dict: """Convert the report card to a readable format.""" return { "overall_score": round(self.overall_score, 4), "level": self.level.value, "context_overlap_score": round(self.context_overlap_score, 4), "semantic_similarity_score": round(self.semantic_similarity_score, 4), "source_attribution_score": round(self.source_attribution_score, 4), }
