The Monster Resort Hotel

Docker Compose as a Hotel Building -- 6 departments, 6 characters, one building that opens and closes together

1

The Hotel Building

Docker Compose is the hotel building itself. Each service is a department inside it. One key opens the whole building. One key locks it all up.

The Big Idea docker-compose up -d = Opening the hotel for the day. Every department powers on, staff take their positions, the building hums to life.
docker-compose down = Closing for the night. Lights off, doors locked, the building goes quiet. But the filing cabinets keep their papers (volumes persist).

The Front Desk
Big Bird manages the API (FastAPI :8000)
▼
Big Bird: "Welcome to Monster Resort! I am the very first face you see when you walk through those big revolving doors. Every single guest -- every request, every question, every complaint -- comes to me first. I stand right here at my desk, I smile, and I say: How can I help you today?"
Hotel Analogy The Front Desk is the ground floor, right by the entrance. Every guest walks up to Big Bird. He checks them in, looks up their reservation, routes them to the right room. He never leaves until the Filing Cabinet Room and the Speed Shelf are both open -- he refuses to stand at his desk unless his support systems are ready.
What This Really Is The FastAPI application. It handles every HTTP request. It exposes /health, /chat, /metrics, and all other endpoints. It depends on Postgres and Redis -- it literally will not start until both report healthy.
docker-compose.yml -- api service 
api:
  build: .
  ports:
    - "8000:8000"
  environment:
    - MRC_DATABASE_URL=postgresql://monster:monster_secret@postgres:5432/monster_resort
    - MRC_REDIS_URL=redis://redis:6379/0
  depends_on:
    postgres:
      condition: service_healthy   # Won't open desk until filing room is ready
    redis:
      condition: service_healthy   # Won't open desk until speed shelf is stocked
  healthcheck:
    test: ["CMD", "curl", "-f", "http://localhost:8000/health"]
    interval: 30s
  restart: unless-stopped

The Count is always the first to arrive and the last to leave.
What This Really Is PostgreSQL 16. The persistent relational database. Stores bookings, sessions, user data. The postgres_data volume means data survives docker-compose down. The healthcheck runs pg_isready every 10 seconds. Nothing else starts until this reports healthy.
docker-compose.yml -- postgres service 
postgres:
  image: postgres:16-alpine
  environment:
    POSTGRES_DB: monster_resort
    POSTGRES_USER: monster
    POSTGRES_PASSWORD: monster_secret
  ports:
    - "5432:5432"
  volumes:
    - postgres_data:/var/lib/postgresql/data  # Filing cabinets survive the night
  healthcheck:
    test: ["CMD-SHELL", "pg_isready -U monster"]
    interval: 10s
    retries: 5

Hotel Analogy Right next to the front desk, within arm's reach, is a small speed shelf. What This Really Is Redis 7. An in-memory cache. Stores recently fetched data for fast retrieval. The API checks Redis first before hitting Postgres. TTL (time-to-live) means cached items expire automatically. The healthcheck runs redis-cli ping -- if Redis says PONG, the shelf is stocked and ready.
docker-compose.yml -- redis service 
redis:
  image: redis:7-alpine
  ports:
    - "6379:6379"
  volumes:
    - redis_data:/data              # Some cookies survive the night
  healthcheck:
    test: ["CMD", "redis-cli", "ping"]  # "PONG!" = shelf is ready
    interval: 10s
    retries: 5

Hotel Analogy In the lobby, mounted on the wall where everyone can see it, is a massive tally board. The Count walks over to the Front Desk every 15 seconds, counts everything happening, and chalks the numbers onto the board. He tracks how many guests arrived this hour, how long they waited, how many errors occurred. The board is always updating. It does not store records permanently -- it stores trends. "How is today going compared to yesterday?"
What This Really Is Prometheus. A metrics collection system. Every 15 seconds it scrapes the /metrics endpoint on the API. It stores time-series data: request counts, response latencies, error rates, memory usage. It does not trigger alerts itself -- it just records. Grafana reads from this board.
docker-compose.yml -- prometheus service 
prometheus:
  image: prom/prometheus:latest
  ports:
    - "9090:9090"
  volumes:
    - ./prometheus.yml:/etc/prometheus/prometheus.yml  # The counting rules
    - prometheus_data:/prometheus                      # The tally records
  command:
    - '--config.file=/etc/prometheus/prometheus.yml'
  restart: unless-stopped

What This Really Is Grafana. A visualization dashboard. It connects to Prometheus as a data source and renders charts, graphs, and alerts. The admin password is set via environment variable. It stores dashboard configurations in a volume so your custom layouts survive restarts.
docker-compose.yml -- grafana service 
grafana:
  image: grafana/grafana:latest
  ports:
    - "3000:3000"

The Experiment Lab
Grover manages MLflow (:5000)
▼
Grover: "I, Grover, the lovable and hardworking monster, run the Experiment Lab in the back of the hotel! When the manager wants to try a new way of greeting guests, or test whether a different breakfast menu makes people happier, they come to ME. I set up the experiment, I run it, I write down EXACTLY what happened -- which version we tested, what the results were, whether it was better or worse. I keep a notebook of every experiment we have ever tried so we never repeat a bad idea!"
Hotel Analogy In the back of the third floor, behind a door marked "EXPERIMENTS IN PROGRESS," is Grover's lab. It is full of notebooks, clipboards, and carefully labeled test results. When management wants to compare two different check-in scripts, or test whether a new concierge approach works better, Grover runs the test, records every detail, and reports back. The lab works independently -- it does not affect the guests at all. It just observes and records.
What This Really Is MLflow. An experiment tracking server. It logs ML evaluation runs -- which model was used, what parameters were set, what the accuracy/latency/cost metrics were. It stores everything in a SQLite database and saves artifacts (model files, reports) to a volume. The API can log experiments to MLflow without MLflow being required to serve guests.
docker-compose.yml -- mlflow service 
mlflow:
  image: ghcr.io/mlflow/mlflow:latest
  ports:
    - "5000:5000"
  command:
    - mlflow
    - server
    - --host=0.0.0.0
    - --port=5000
    - --backend-store-uri=sqlite:///mlflow/mlflow.db   # Grover's notebook
    - --default-artifact-root=/mlflow/artifacts        # Shelf of test results
  volumes:
    - mlflow_data:/mlflow                               # Lab notebooks survive the night
  restart: unless-stopped
The Storage Closets (Volumes) At the very bottom of the docker-compose file, five storage closets are declared: postgres_data, redis_data, prometheus_data, grafana_data, mlflow_data. These are physical closets that survive the night. When the hotel closes (docker-compose down), the closets stay locked and full. When it reopens, everything is still there. Only docker-compose down -v empties the closets -- that is like calling in a demolition crew.

Opening the Hotel

The morning routine. docker-compose up -d starts the building, but departments open in a strict order.

The Building Manager (Docker Compose): "Good morning, everyone. It is 6:00 AM. Time to open Monster Resort. Now, I cannot just flip every switch at once -- that would be chaos. Big Bird cannot greet guests if the filing cabinets are locked. The tally board means nothing if there is no desk to count. We open in ORDER."
Step 1: Turn on the Basement Lights
Count von Count: "I arrive first! Always first! I walk down to the basement, I unlock the filing cabinet room, I turn on every fluorescent light -- one! two! three! four! Ah ah ah! -- and then I check every cabinet drawer. Can I open them? Can I read the files? I pull one out, I check the label. Yes! Everything is in order. I pick up the phone and call the building manager: 'The filing room is READY. You may send down the next person.'"
What Actually Happens Postgres container starts. It initializes the database, runs recovery if needed. Every 10 seconds, the healthcheck runs pg_isready -U monster. Only when this returns success does Docker mark it as healthy. Nothing that depends_on: postgres: condition: service_healthy will start until this passes.
↓
Step 2: Stock the Speed Shelf
Cookie Monster: "Me come in next! Me wipe down shelf, me check it clean, me put out fresh labels. Then building manager call me on phone and say: 'Cookie Monster, is shelf ready?' And me say: 'PONG!' That secret code word. If me say PONG, it mean shelf ready for cookies -- I mean, for data. Om nom nom."
What Actually Happens Redis container starts. It loads any persisted data from the volume. The healthcheck runs redis-cli ping every 10 seconds. When Redis responds with PONG, it is marked healthy. This happens in parallel with Postgres -- both are independent, both just need to pass their own healthchecks.
↓
Step 3: Open the Front Desk
Big Bird: "I have been waiting in the hallway, pacing back and forth, checking my watch. The building manager will NOT let me go to my desk until he gets TWO phone calls: one from the Count saying the filing room is ready, and one from Cookie Monster saying PONG. Only THEN does the manager unlock the front door and say: 'Big Bird, take your position.' I walk to my desk, I straighten my tie, I put out the guest register, and I call the manager: 'The front desk is OPEN.' Oh, what a beautiful morning!"
What Actually Happens The API container waits because of depends_on with condition: service_healthy for both Postgres and Redis. Docker does not even start the API container until both dependencies are healthy. Once started, the API connects to Postgres and Redis using environment variable URLs, runs its own healthcheck (curl -f http://localhost:8000/health), and begins accepting requests.
↓
Step 4: The Tally Board Starts Ticking
Count von Count: "Now that Big Bird is at his desk, I can begin my SECOND job! I walk to the lobby, I pick up my chalk, I look at my pocket watch, and I begin. Every fifteen seconds: I walk to the front desk, I count everything happening, I walk back to my board, I write the numbers. Walk, count, write. Walk, count, write. I will do this all day. I will NEVER stop. This is the best job in the world!"
What Actually Happens Prometheus starts independently (no depends_on in the compose file). It reads prometheus.yml which tells it to scrape http://api:8000/metrics every 15 seconds. If the API is not ready yet, the first few scrapes fail silently and Prometheus just retries on the next interval. No crash, no panic -- just empty tally marks until the desk opens.
↓
Step 5: Management Checks the Display
Elmo: "Elmo is here! Elmo turns on the big screen on the second floor! But Elmo's screen is blank at first -- Elmo needs to connect to the Count's tally board so Elmo can read the numbers and make pretty pictures. Once Elmo sees the numbers flowing in, Elmo paints the lines green -- that means everything is wonderful! Elmo will keep painting all day! La la la!"
What Actually Happens Grafana starts independently. On first run, you configure it to use Prometheus as a data source (URL: http://prometheus:9090). It loads saved dashboards from the volume. If Prometheus has not started yet, dashboards show "No data" until the connection is established. No crash -- just empty charts until the tally board has numbers.
↓
Step 6: The Experiment Lab Opens
Grover: "I, Grover, arrive last -- and that is perfectly fine! My lab does not affect the guests at all. I turn on my lab lights, I open my notebook to the last page, I sharpen my pencil, and I sit down at my desk. I am ready. Whenever the manager wants to test something new, they send a note to my lab and I begin the experiment. But until then? I sit here, organized and prepared, like the adorable and patient monster that I am."
What Actually Happens MLflow starts independently. It launches a web server on port 5000, connects to its SQLite backend, and waits for experiment logging calls from the API. It is a passive service -- it does not reach out to anyone. The API calls MLflow when it wants to log an evaluation run. If MLflow is not running, the API simply skips experiment tracking.
The One Command That Does All of This docker-compose up -d

The -d flag means "detached" -- the building manager opens everything and then goes to his office. He does not stand in the lobby watching. The hotel runs on its own. If you want to watch the opening happen in real time, drop the -d: docker-compose up and you will see every log message from every department as they power on.

A Guest Arrives

A single HTTP request walks through all six departments. Follow the guest from the front door to checkout.

The Scenario
A monster named Zoe walks through the front door. She says: "I would like to check in. My name is Zoe, and I have a reservation." This is a POST /chat request with the message "I'd like to check in."
Zoe walks up to the Front Desk
Big Bird: "Oh! Hello, Zoe! Welcome to Monster Resort! Let me help you check in. First, let me look at my speed shelf to see if you have checked in recently -- sometimes guests come back within the same day and I already have their information handy."
What Happens The FastAPI endpoint receives the POST request. It parses the message, identifies the intent, and prepares to look up Zoe's information. First stop: the cache.
↓
Big Bird checks the Speed Shelf
Big Bird: "Cookie Monster, do you have anything for Zoe on the shelf?"
Cookie Monster: "Me look... me look... Nope! Shelf no have Zoe. She new today. Big Bird need to go to basement."
What Happens The API checks Redis for a cached session or previous response for this user. Cache MISS -- nothing found. The API must now query the database.
↓
Big Bird sends a note to the Filing Cabinet Room
Big Bird: *writes a note, puts it in the pneumatic tube, pulls the lever* "Count! I need the reservation for Zoe, please!"
Count von Count: *catches the capsule, opens it, reads the note, walks to cabinet Z, pulls drawer 14, flips through folders* "Zoe... Zoe... AH HA! ONE reservation for Zoe! Checked in at 3:00 PM, Room 442, two nights, breakfast included! I shall send this back!" *loads the folder into the tube, pulls the lever* "One reservation FOUND! Ah ah ah!"
What Happens The API sends a SQL query to PostgreSQL: SELECT * FROM bookings WHERE guest_name = 'Zoe'. Postgres finds the record and returns it. The API now has the data it needs.
↓
Big Bird stashes a copy on the Speed Shelf
Big Bird: "Cookie Monster, hold onto a copy of Zoe's info on the shelf. If she comes back to ask about her room number or breakfast time, I want to grab it fast without running to the basement again."
Cookie Monster: *carefully places the card on the shelf* "Me got it! But me only keep it for thirty minutes. After that, it go stale like old cookie. Big Bird have to ask basement again."
What Happens The API writes the result to Redis with a TTL (time-to-live). Next time this data is requested, the API finds it in Redis (cache HIT) and skips the Postgres query entirely. After the TTL expires, the cache entry is automatically deleted.
↓
Big Bird responds to Zoe
Big Bird: "Wonderful news, Zoe! I have your reservation right here. Room 442, two nights, breakfast included. Here is your key. The elevator is to your left. Welcome to Monster Resort!"
What Happens The API formats the response and sends the HTTP response back to the client. Status 200, JSON body with the chat response. The guest's request is complete.
↓
Meanwhile, in the Background...
Count von Count: *glances at pocket watch, walks to front desk* "It has been fifteen seconds! Let me count. ONE guest checked in since my last visit. Response time was 0.3 seconds. ZERO errors. Beautiful!" *walks back to tally board, writes the numbers*
Elmo: *sees new numbers appear on the tally board* "Ooh! Elmo sees a new dot on the response time chart! It is green and LOW -- that means the hotel is fast! Elmo draws the line!"
Grover: *sitting quietly in the lab, pencil ready* "No experiment request for me this time. I shall wait. But if the manager decides to A/B test a new greeting style, they know where to find me!"
What Happens Prometheus scrapes /metrics on its next 15-second cycle and picks up the incremented request counter and the recorded latency histogram. Grafana's dashboard auto-refreshes and shows the new data point. MLflow is idle -- it only activates when the API explicitly logs an evaluation experiment.
The Complete Flow in One Sentence Guest arrives at Front Desk (API) → Big Bird checks Speed Shelf (Redis) → cache miss → note to Filing Room (Postgres) → data found → copy stashed on Speed Shelf (Redis) → response to guest → Count tallies the visit (Prometheus) → Elmo paints the chart (Grafana) → Grover waits in the lab (MLflow, idle).
What if Zoe comes back 5 minutes later?
She walks up to Big Bird again. Big Bird reaches over to the Speed Shelf. Cookie Monster has her info ready. Cache HIT. Big Bird never needs to go to the basement. The response is instant. The Count still tallies it. Elmo still paints it. But the trip was shorter -- no basement visit needed.

When a Department Closes

What happens when a single department goes down? Not the whole hotel -- just one room. Each failure has different consequences.

The Building Manager (Docker Compose): "Every department has a restart: unless-stopped policy. That means if a department goes dark, I try to reopen it automatically. But while it is dark, the rest of the hotel feels the impact differently depending on WHICH department went down."
💥
The Filing Cabinet Room Goes Dark
PostgreSQL crashes -- SEVERITY: CRITICAL
▼
Count von Count: *lights flicker, go out* "No! My cabinets! I cannot see! I cannot count! The pneumatic tube is silent! I... I must rest."
Big Bird: *frantically pressing the pneumatic tube button* "Count? COUNT? I need Zoe's reservation! The tube is not responding! I... I can still check the speed shelf for guests who visited recently, but any NEW guest who needs their file? I have to tell them: 'I am sorry, our records room is temporarily unavailable. Please wait.' Oh dear, oh dear."
Impact This is the worst failure. The API can still serve cached responses from Redis, but any request that needs a database lookup fails. New sessions cannot be created. Bookings cannot be retrieved from permanent storage. The API stays running but returns errors for database-dependent operations.
Recovery Docker's restart: unless-stopped automatically restarts the Postgres container. Once it passes the pg_isready healthcheck, the API reconnects. No data is lost -- the volume preserves everything. Typical recovery: 10-30 seconds.
💥
The Speed Shelf Falls Over
Redis crashes -- SEVERITY: MODERATE
▼
Cookie Monster: *shelf wobbles, crashes to the ground, cookies fly everywhere* "NOOO! Me shelf! All the quick-access cards scattered on the floor! Me need to rebuild!"
Big Bird: *looks at the mess, sighs, straightens his tie* "Well... the shelf is down. That means every single question now requires a trip to the basement. It is slower, and the Count is going to be very busy with all those pneumatic tube requests. But we CAN still serve every guest. Nobody gets turned away. It just takes longer."
Impact The hotel is slower but still functional. Every request bypasses the cache and hits Postgres directly. Response times increase. Postgres load increases. But no requests fail -- the API is designed to fall back to the database when Redis is unavailable.
Recovery Docker restarts Redis. Once redis-cli ping returns PONG, the API reconnects. The cache is empty (cold cache) so the first wave of requests will all go to Postgres, but as responses are cached, the shelf gradually restocks itself.
💥
The Front Desk Closes
API crashes -- SEVERITY: TOTAL OUTAGE
▼
Big Bird: *collapses behind the desk, feathers everywhere* "I... I need a moment..."
Count von Count: "The front desk is CLOSED?! But I am still here! My filing cabinets are fine! My tally board is ready! But nobody is sending me notes through the tube because there is nobody at the desk to RECEIVE the guests!"
Cookie Monster: "Me shelf still has cookies on it! But nobody reaching for them! Everything just... sitting here."
Impact Complete outage. No HTTP requests can be served. Guests see connection refused or 502 errors. Prometheus scrapes fail (no /metrics endpoint). Grafana shows gaps in the charts. But Postgres, Redis, and MLflow are all fine -- they are just idle. All data is safe.
Recovery Docker restarts the API container. It waits for Postgres and Redis healthchecks (they are already healthy since they never went down). The API starts, passes its own healthcheck, and resumes serving. Prometheus picks up scraping on the next 15-second cycle. Grafana fills in the gap. Typical recovery: 40-60 seconds (start_period is 40s).
💥
The Tally Board Goes Silent
Prometheus crashes -- SEVERITY: LOW (guests unaffected)
▼
Count von Count: *drops chalk, staggers back from the tally board* "The board! The board has gone dark! I cannot write my numbers! But wait... the filing room is still fine. The guests are still being served. They do not even KNOW the tally board exists. This only affects the managers who like to look at the numbers."
Elmo: "Elmo's screen is frozen! Elmo cannot see new numbers because the Count stopped writing! Elmo's charts have a big gap in them. But Elmo knows the hotel is still open. The guests are fine."
Impact Guests notice nothing. The API serves requests normally. Redis and Postgres are unaffected. The only impact is observability: you lose metrics for the duration of the outage. Grafana shows a gap in charts. When Prometheus restarts, it resumes scraping and the gap remains in the historical data.
💥
The Display Board Goes Black
Grafana crashes -- SEVERITY: MINIMAL
▼
Elmo: *screen flickers and goes black* "Oh no! Elmo's pretty pictures are gone! But... the Count is STILL writing numbers on his tally board. Elmo just cannot paint them right now. When Elmo comes back, ALL the numbers will still be there on the tally board, and Elmo can paint the whole history again! Nothing is lost!"
Impact Almost zero impact. Prometheus is still collecting metrics. All data is still being recorded. You just cannot visualize it until Grafana restarts. No guests are affected. No data is lost. When Grafana comes back, dashboards reload from the volume and Prometheus backfills the charts automatically.
💥
The Experiment Lab Locks Up
MLflow crashes -- SEVERITY: MINIMAL
▼
Grover: *lab door slams shut, lock clicks* "Oh dear! I, Grover, am locked out of my own laboratory! But the guests will not notice. They do not even know this room exists. The only problem is that if the manager wants to start a new experiment right now, they cannot. But all my previous notebooks are safe inside -- they will be there when I get back in."
Impact Near zero impact. The API continues serving all requests. It simply skips the MLflow logging step. Previous experiment data is safe in the volume. When MLflow restarts, it loads its SQLite database and all previous experiments are intact. New experiments can resume.
Failure Severity Ranking 1. API down = Total outage. Nobody gets in the front door.
2. Postgres down = Critical. New guests cannot be looked up. Cached data still works.
3. Redis down = Moderate. Everything works but slower. All requests hit the database.
4. Prometheus down = Low. Guests unaffected. You lose visibility into metrics.
5. Grafana down = Minimal. Data still collected. You just cannot see the charts.
6. MLflow down = Minimal. Experiments paused. All guest services unaffected.
What about docker-compose down?
The Building Manager: "That is not a failure -- that is a planned closing. I walk through the building in reverse order. I close the experiment lab. I turn off the display. I stop the tally board. I close the front desk. I lock the speed shelf. And finally, I turn off the lights in the basement. Everyone goes home. But the storage closets (volumes) stay locked and full. Tomorrow morning, docker-compose up -d, and we do it all again."
