Before Opening — make_registry(db, pdf, rag)

Before the hotel opens each night, Big Bird wheels three pieces of equipment to three service desks and chains them in place. Until the equipment arrives, the extension buttons are dead. Once wired, they light up.

Big Bird — Before Sundown

Every evening, before the first guest calls, Big Bird arrives with a rolling cart. On it: a filing cabinet, a printer, and a library key. He wheels each piece to its assigned desk and chains it with a padlock.

The Booking Desk gets the filing cabinet AND the printer. The Lookup Desk gets just the filing cabinet. The Information Desk gets just the library key. Only then does he flip the master switch — and the extension buttons on the switchboard light up.

Before Big Bird finishes, if someone picks up the phone and dials an extension, nothing happens. The button is dark. The desk has nothing to work with. The equipment IS the desk's ability to function.

The Dispatcher — Always On Duty

The dispatcher doesn't know HOW anything works. He just routes calls, times them, and writes everything down. If something breaks, he catches the fall — gives the guest a polite message and writes the real story in his private notebook.

The Dispatcher — Front Desk, Always On Duty

The dispatcher sits at the front desk with a stopwatch, a ledger, and the switchboard. Every call that comes in goes through him first. He doesn't know how bookings work. He doesn't know how the filing cabinet is organized. He doesn't care.

His job: route the call to the right desk, start the stopwatch, make a tally mark, wait for the result, stop the stopwatch, and write everything in the log. If a desk clerk trips and drops something, the dispatcher doesn't scream. He calmly tells the guest "We're experiencing a small issue" and writes the real error in his private log.

1
Ticket Number
The dispatcher tears off a ticket from the roll and writes a number on it. Every call gets its own ticket, so later he can look up exactly what happened with that call.
2
Directory Check
He looks at the switchboard. "Is this extension even real?" If someone dials an extension that doesn't exist, he says "Sorry, no such service" and hangs up. No point routing a call to a desk that isn't there.
3
Tidy Up the Message
Sometimes guests mumble or add extra words. The dispatcher cleans up the message — straightens out the request so the desk clerk gets a clear, neat version.
4
START THE STOPWATCH
Click! The stopwatch starts. From this moment, every second is being counted.
5
Tally Mark
He walks over to the big tally board on the lobby wall and makes a mark under the right column. "That's the fourth booking call today!" Now the hotel knows which desks are busiest.
6
Connect the Call
He plugs the call into the right desk on the switchboard and waits. The desk clerk picks up, does the work, and hands back the result. While he's waiting, the dispatcher can still answer other incoming calls.
7
STOP THE STOPWATCH
Click! "That took 47 milliseconds!" he announces to no one in particular, scribbling the number in his ledger.
8
Write It All Down
In his ledger, he writes: which desk handled it, what the guest asked for, how long it took, and whether it worked or not. A complete record of every call, every time.

What if something goes wrong?

💥
The desk clerk at the Booking Desk drops the filing cabinet. Papers fly everywhere. The request can't be completed.
🎭
The dispatcher catches it. He doesn't let the chaos reach the guest. He steps in front of the mess.
🛎️
He calmly picks up the phone: "We're experiencing a small issue with your request. We'll look into it." Polite. Professional. The guest never hears the crash.
📓
In his private ledger, he writes the real story: what went wrong, which desk it was, the ticket number, and how far along the call was when it broke. Everything needed to fix it later.
⏱️
The stopwatch still gets recorded — even for failed calls. He knows exactly how long it ran before the crash. That timing helps figure out what went wrong.

1
Check In

In the Room
Dracula chats with the concierge. Every message gets written on the notepad. When the notepad hits 12 pages, housekeeping comes in — writes a short summary on the desk card, rips out the old pages. Fresh notepad, summary saved.
← The room you already understand.
3
The Phone Call
Dracula: "Book a room for Nosferatu!" This goes on the notepad too — it's still a message. The concierge reads the notepad and thinks: "I can't do this from the room. I need to call downstairs."
4
The Guest Services Card
The concierge picks up the Guest Services card on the nightstand — the laminated one that lists all three extensions. She sees Extension 1: Booking Desk. She fills in the request: who's it for, which hotel, what dates.
The Guest Services card tells the concierge what's available and what information each desk needs.
5
Downstairs to the Service Desk
The dispatcher picks up. Starts the stopwatch. Makes a tally mark. Routes the call to the Booking Desk. The desk clerk checks the approved hotel list, files the booking in the cabinet, prints the receipt. Result travels back upstairs.
Same 8 steps as always: ticket, check, stopwatch, tally, route, wait, stop, log.
6
Back in the Room
The result comes back to the room: "Booking confirmed! Here's your receipt." The concierge writes this on the notepad too. Now the notepad has Dracula's original request AND the answer from downstairs. Both are just messages. Both will be summarized by housekeeping eventually.
The notepad doesn't care where the answer came from — it's just another line on the page.
7
The Cycle
Chat, tool call, result, more chat, another tool call. The notepad fills up. Housekeeping runs. The desk card is updated. The room and the service desk work together, forever, one message at a time.
